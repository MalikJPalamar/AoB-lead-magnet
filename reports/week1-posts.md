## Post 2 (Wednesday) - Contrarian Take
**Optimal Posting Time:** 10:00 AM EST

---

Everyone is excited about AI agents. I'm worried. Here's why 90% will fail.

Let me explain.

Right now, every startup is slapping "agent" on their product. Investors are throwing money at anything that moves autonomously. But I've seen this movie before.

The 90% that will fail share one trait: **They automate before they validate.**

Here's the pattern:
1. Build an agent that can do X
2. Assume people want to automate X
3. Skip the part where you check if X actually needs automating
4. Wonder why adoption is zero

The agents that win won't be the most autonomous. They'll be the ones that solve problems people actually have - and prove it before scaling.

I've made this mistake. Twice. It cost me $2M and 3 years.

Comment if you've experienced this - would love to hear what's working for you.

## Post 4 (Friday) - Weekly Reflection
**Optimal Posting Time:** 2:00 PM EST

---

Friday thought: The biggest AI opportunity no one is talking about.

Everyone's focused on:
- GPT-5 rumors
- Agent frameworks
- Coding assistants

But the real opportunity is boring. It's unsexy. It's:

**AI that helps people make decisions, not AI that replaces decisions.**

The founders who will win in 2026 aren't building autocomplete. They're building clarity.

Think about it:
- Doctors need help weighing treatment options, not a robot performing surgery
- Executives need help seeing trade-offs, not AI running the company
- Parents need help planning, not AI raising kids

The gap isn't capability. It's trust. And trust comes from AI that augments human judgment, not bypasses it.

Hot take? Maybe. But I've been wrong before (see: my 3 failed products).

What do you think - am I onto something or just tired on a Friday?
